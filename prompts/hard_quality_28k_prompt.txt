You are writing a long-form execution blueprint for a company-wide transformation program.
This document is used by executives, engineering directors, product leads, finance, security, and operations managers.
Your output must be detailed, coherent, and directly actionable.

Program objective:
Deliver profitable growth, reliability, and operational discipline in one integrated plan.

Company profile:
- Mid-sized AI product company.
- Three product surfaces: consumer assistant, enterprise workflow suite, benchmarking platform.
- Shared infrastructure, shared on-call, partially shared planning process.

Observed challenges:
1) High compute spend variance from ungoverned benchmark workloads.
2) Product commitments made before engineering capacity validation.
3) Reliability outcomes improving in pockets but not system-wide.
4) Security reviews are often late-stage and create launch delays.
5) Finance and engineering disagree on cost attribution granularity.
6) Enterprise sales commitments drift from platform roadmap.
7) Data quality and metric definitions differ by team.
8) Incident response is faster, but repeat incidents are not dropping fast enough.
9) Internal platform tooling is heavily used but under-owned.
10) Hiring pipeline cannot keep pace with specialized role demand.

You must build:
- Strategic narrative
- Operating model
- Governance model
- Financial envelope model
- Reliability and risk architecture
- Metrics design
- Quarter-by-quarter implementation plan

Required sections:

Section A: North-star outcomes
- Define 5 program outcomes with measurable success criteria.
- For each: timeline, owner group, dependency class.

Section B: Assumptions and constraints
- Hard constraints (budget, headcount, capacity, compliance).
- Soft constraints (preferences, sequencing).
- Unknowns requiring discovery.

Section C: Risk decomposition
- At least 12 risks.
- Include root cause, trigger, blast radius, early warning signal, and mitigation.
- Include at least 3 systemic risks with cross-team coupling.

Section D: Operating model redesign
- How planning cycles should run.
- How intake and prioritization should be gated.
- What decision rights move to which level.
- How escalation should work.

Section E: Delivery architecture
- Program split into workstreams with interfaces.
- For each workstream: charter, non-goals, deliverables, acceptance criteria.
- Include explicit handoff contracts between teams.

Section F: Financial and compute controls
- Budgeting model by function and workload class.
- Spend guardrails for benchmark workloads.
- Approval policy for overages.
- Monthly variance review template.

Section G: Reliability and security integration
- Release policy tied to reliability thresholds.
- Security-by-default integration policy.
- Incident recurrence prevention loop.
- Runbook and ownership completeness targets.

Section H: KPI framework
- Leading indicators and lagging indicators.
- Metric definitions to avoid ambiguity.
- KPI anti-patterns to avoid.
- Review cadence and corrective action triggers.

Section I: 4-quarter roadmap
- Quarter objectives, milestones, dependencies, and decision gates.
- Include explicit "stop doing" decisions.
- Include expected outcomes and failure contingencies.

Section J: Scenario planning
- Base case, optimistic case, stress case.
- For each: assumptions, adaptation triggers, and fallback actions.

Section K: Weekly execution ritual
- Agenda template for cross-functional review.
- Decision log format.
- Owner accountability checklist.

Section L: Immediate actions (next 2 weeks)
- List concrete actions with owners and expected outputs.

Strict constraints:
- No external references.
- No buzzword-heavy filler.
- Every recommendation must map to at least one measurable outcome.
- Explicitly call out tradeoffs.
- Keep the plan implementable with realistic coordination overhead.

Input notes:
- Consumer users value speed but leave when quality is inconsistent.
- Enterprise buyers prioritize reliability and operational predictability.
- Benchmarking platform improves internal quality but causes cost spikes when unmanaged.
- Leadership wants one plan, not three disconnected plans.
- Team sentiment indicates fatigue from changing priorities and unclear ownership.

Output style:
- Structured headings and compact tables.
- Direct, practical language.
- Final section: “Approval Decisions Needed Now”.

Now produce the complete transformation blueprint.
